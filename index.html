🟡 SOCIETY · LEGAL REFORM

Japan's Joint Custody Law Takes Effect — Divorced Couples Can Now Share Custody, Ending Japan's Status as Only G7 Nation Without It

Tap to expand

What: A landmark revision to Japan's Civil Code took effect on April 1, allowing family courts to grant joint custody to divorcing couples. Japan was previously the only G7 country without joint custody. Why this happened and why it matters: (1) Years of domestic and international criticism — including high-profile cases where foreign parents accused Japanese ex-spouses of "child abduction" by cutting off all access — created mounting pressure; (2) The revision also mandates ¥20,000/month minimum child support, previously unenforceable; (3) Courts can still grant sole custody in cases of domestic violence — addressing concerns from women's groups that joint custody could force victims to maintain ties with abusers; (4) Parents who divorced under the old system can petition for custody review — potentially reopening thousands of cases. This is one of the most significant family law reforms in Japan in decades.
