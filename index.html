Japan Retrieves Rare-Earth Mud From 6,000m Deep Pacific Seabed ‚Äî Major Step Toward Reducing China Dependence

In a potentially game-changing development, Japan's Cabinet Office and JAMSTEC announced Monday they successfully retrieved mud believed to contain rare-earth elements from 6,000 meters beneath the Pacific Ocean near Minamitorishima ‚Äî Japan's easternmost island, ~1,950 km southeast of Tokyo. The achievement is driven by Japan's urgent need to reduce its near-total dependence on Chinese rare earths (China controls ~60% of global mining and ~90% of processing), which has become a critical national security vulnerability as Beijing tightens export restrictions. The deep-sea scientific drilling vessel Chikyu collected the seabed slurry, which will now be analyzed for key elements used in EV motors, wind turbines, and defense systems. If commercially viable, Japan's Pacific seabed deposits could contain centuries of global rare earth supply ‚Äî though extraction at this depth remains years from industrial scale.
